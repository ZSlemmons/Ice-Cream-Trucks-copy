Does New York City Hate Icecream?

New Yorkers vs. Icecream Trucks: A battle for NYC's streets

By Zoe Slemmons

Here in New York City, ice cream is a serious business. The frozen confection’s history in the city is nearly as old as the nation itself, dating back to Philip Lenzi’s sweet shop, which began advertising ice cream in 1777.

But that long history has often been marked by controversy, often resulting in regualtions. A long forgotten state law makes it illegal to carry ice cream in your back pocket on a Sunday. Another bans eating ice cream at bus stops.

More recently, the ice cream truck industry has been at the center of multiple scandals. In 2013, a years-long turf war erupted between top vendors, Mister Softee and New York Ice Cream. In 2019, the city launched an initiative aimed at cracking down on ice cream truck owners’ use of shell companies to avoid paying more than $4.5 million in traffic violation fines. The program, aptly dubbed “Operation Meltdown,” resulted in the seizure of 46 ice cream trucks.

But perhaps the most enduring conflict in ice cream’s storied history has been the battle between every-day New Yorkers and the ice cream truck jingle.

According to records from NYC Open Data, city residents have lodged more than 16,000 noise complaints against ice cream trucks in the past 10 years, with an average of 1,500 complaints per year. 311 calls peaked in 2020 when the COVID-19 pandemic sent the city into lockdown, forcing many New Yorkers to work from home.

So where do all these ice cream hating New Yorkers live? An analysis by borough, identified Manhattan as the epicenter for the highest number of complaints, closely followed by Brooklyn.

While the prevalence of noise complaints in densely-populated Manhattan may not come as a shock, many New Yorkers may be surprised by the neighborhood breakdown, which shows Washington Heights as the area with the highest number of calls to 311.

A working class neighborhood in Upper Manhattan, Washington Heights is bounded by 155th Street to the south, Dyckman Street to the north, and the Hudson and Harlem Rivers to the west and east respectively.

Historically a majority Latino community, Washington Heights is better known for its piraguas – traditional Puerto Rican shaved ice treat (featured in Lin Manuel Miranda’s musical and 2021 film, In the Heights) – than for its ice cream. Nevertheless, the neighborhood has lodged more than 900 ice cream truck noise complaints since 2015. The area far outpaced the next highest Manhattan neighborhoods, Midtown and nearby Harlem, which both had a little over 500 calls.

Despite the many complaints, records show that only 13 of the Washington Heights calls resulted in a summons – only 1.4%.

Citywide, the rate of summons is even lower. In the past decade, only 122 summons have been issued for jingle infractions. That’s less than 1% of the total complaints.

It’s a strikingly low rate of return compared with other noise complaints investigated by the city’s Department of Environmental Protection. Data from the same time period showed that construction-related noise complaints, which account for more than half of the complaints received by DEP, resulted in summons about 2% of the time. While still low, that is more than twice the citywide rate for ice cream trucks.

Due to their mobile nature, ice cream trucks may be particularly difficult to catch in the act of violating noise oridinances. And so, for those tormented by their persistent jingles, the battle against this elusive foe continues.
